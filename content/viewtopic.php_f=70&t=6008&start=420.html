by drsmooth » Sat Aug 02, 2008 18:39:02

Laexile wrote:I didn't say "we" are the government.

of course not. I did. And of course who said it is not relevant. The question, which you needn't actively address, is whether "we" - y'know, as in "we, the people" - are, ultimately, the government.

Laexile wrote:Incomes of the bottom 90% increased 4.6% during this period. What would be a sufficient increase?

my but we're handy with figures today. Did you think to cut/paste the incomes increase of the other 10% during "this period" (I don't recall a a period being defined, my apologies)? No? Hmmm.

Laexile wrote:I thought this was a country where we celebrated people working hard and earning more money.

Party on dude - just consider it ain't a constitutional requirement or anything. And it may be incidental to you, but consider that significant #s of people in that under-documented top 10% are not working as hard as you for their disproportionate share, however you want to define "hard" work (in the case of that upper 10% your definition will pretty much have to explain that it consists mostly of relationship management. Of course they have the 'right' relationships, or can fake them, and you don't/can't).

They - and you - definitely work harder than me, no argument there.

Laxile wrote:Are you suggesting capping the amount of money people can make?

What, and fit neatly into a slot on your narrow spectrum of possible opinions? Not on your life.

Who really cares about earnings, anyway? I'd rather talk about ownership, or control of property, or whatever you want to call the power to make decisions about the production of "valued" stuff (tangible & intangible) and the externalities that go with that - and whether some are more 'entitled' to that power than others; if so, what the terms are of that superior entitlement; what the 'right' due process is for the setting & revision of such terms; whether adequate forms of such due process are currently in place anywhere; etc.

Laexile wrote:Throwing road blocks in front of businesses to bring their revenues down? ....[unfocused digression]....Of course history shows that anything that impedes businesses hurts their employees also.


Right.

What?

"history shows" a lot of things. History shows that people successfully make lots of "economic" compromises that "impede" businesses and "hurt" their employees, but that people endure, and maybe even prevail. The turn of the 20th century workplace safety measures instituted in the wake of the Triangle Shirtwaist fire undoubtedly were a pain in the ass for like enterprises, and even meant some poor women missed out on a great career opportunity. Somehow NYC's and New York State's, and the United States economy managed to survive such impediments - to thrive, even. However does that score anything like an effective point for whatever case you're trying to make?

You make so many assumptions about the way things are that you seem unable to imagine the mutability of the forces that underlie them (things as they are, that is).

Property rights, ownership rights, economic relationships are not the result of natural laws - they're social constructs, human compromises, with more room to change, to be changed, than we conventionally imagine. Why not imagine them then? What's it cost you?
