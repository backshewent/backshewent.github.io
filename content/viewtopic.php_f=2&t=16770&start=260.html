Ethan Canin's A Doubter's Almanac took a long time to get where it was going but was ultimately worthwhile. You don't have to know a lot about math (I certainly don't) to enjoy it, but if you do certain parts will probably be a lot more entertaining. A lot of critical reviews are about how the main character treats and regards women (poorly) and how his son (who narrates the second half of the book) fights to avoid this but in some ways continues it. I don't quite get the criticism since it's one of the main themes of the book and the author obviously recognizes and doesn't endorse it. But I can understand how the behavior would be objectionable. Recently finished two books that do NOT have shit in their title, but are essentially about the world going to shit, or becoming dominated by humankind, which itself may be going to shit: Martin Ford's The Rise of the Robots and Yuval Harari's Sapiens.

2nd is better-written; 1st is more ... pragmatic, though Valley denizen Ford is predictably squeamish about the economic implications of his principal thesis, which, to simplify, is that the contribution that people make to production processes is dwindling, and soon enough won't amount to much.

One of Harari's themes is a favorite of mine; that humankind's ability not merely to imagine, but to give fealty to concepts made mostly of our imagination - stuff like religion, and money, and "corporate persons" - is practically indispensible to why we've thrived as a species.

Starting Adam Hochschild's Spain in Our Hearts: Americans in the Spanish Civil War. It's one of those historical periods, and a collection of historical characters, I only know from too many old movies.

The story of the defense of a doomed republic seemed in the spirit of our current mean season.
