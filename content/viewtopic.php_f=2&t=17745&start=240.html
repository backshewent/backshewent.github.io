I think you're mostly right here, as there doesn't seem to be a finite amount of political capital to spend over a term. Everything careens from issue to issue, and there doesn't seem to be a ton of carryover. Between sending signals to their base that they're willing to fight Trump and it's always easier when the other side breaks some institutional norm so you don't have to when it's your turn, I don't think filibustering is a bad option overall. They'll have a bit of trouble because some of the red state Dems up in 2018 will make noise clamoring for a vote, so it won't be a clear partisan fight, but that won't be the main story.

Where it might backfire is circumstances could change that makes it impossible for McConnell to trigger the nuclear option on future nominees, while he doesn't look likely to have that problem on this one. Collins seems to be the most likely to oppose it now, but all signs point to them being able to get to 51 if need be. If a future nominee is more controversial, if a GOP member passes away or resigns, if the GOP loses seats in 2018 (unlikely, but who knows), etc. we might end up in a place where McConnell can't get a majority on it. This isn't something that's more likely than not to happen, but it's feasible, and Dems might do well to have this in their back pocket in case a seat comes open that would change the balance of the court.
