by Wolfgang622 » Thu Feb 23, 2017 13:32:30

On the soda thing, I do know this from having worked in the supermarket industry, that they really lean on soda as a money-maker - at least when it comes to how they present their business model.

What do I mean? Well, I distinctly remember watching a training video in which we new employees were told that a bottle of soda that would have retailed (at that time) for one dollar would cost the company $100 to replace, if lost to "shrink" - retail for anything that can happen to a product before it can be sold at retail, but for which the company has paid: contamination, damage, and most importantly, theft.

This, of course, must be absurd, and was a nice lesson to young me that figures lie and liars figure: if each $1 bottle of soda represented a potential loss of $100, meaning that for each lost bottle 100 would need to be sold to replace it, it would be senseless to carry the product. This is a game of averages across the entire company, including the salaries and benefits for loss prevention staff, weighed in such a way that all this spending was to prevent the theft of a single bottle of soda.

I resented them lying to me (but I worked there anyway). And now, I smell another rat, regarding Brantt's ShopRite layoff story (I used to frequent the South Philly locations): seems like either their business is down overall, or someone up the food chain figures he can just divvy up bigger bonuses for himself and a few of his friends if he lays off some staff, and the soda tax provides effective cover for why they "have" to let go of staff due to factors beyond their control.
