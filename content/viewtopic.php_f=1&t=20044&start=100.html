by mcare89 » Sun May 17, 2020 19:20:18

Eem wrote:This is bothering me: https://twitter.com/Alden_Gonzalez/stat ... 2113457152

Everybody is screaming about how baseball needs to make sure it's safe if it's going to return. So baseball likely goes into complete overkill mode on its policy -- making sure it's safe and probably even multiple degrees beyond that -- and because it is so detailed, there's an argument that it's not safe to return.

It's kind of like how doing more tests reveals more cases.

I agree with you in principle, I think the baseball plan is essentially an ultra-strict version of what the product will actually look like. If you're testing regularly, I don't know that you have to enforce some of these stricter policies, particularly when other sports are likely going to come back that are literal contact sports.

There's a very fine line between taking out the "that's the way things have always been done in baseball" things that are probably dumb to do in this environment (spitting seeds, clubhouse buffet spreads, high fives) and some of the more impactful changes (everyone has to sit in the stands away from each other, fielders should stay away from on-base players) that actively impact the way the game is played.

But I do think that if you do too much to cross that line and you feel that some of those more impactful changes to the sport are necessary to play, it is worth asking the question. I do think baseball just put forward the most health-conscious version of their plan that's possible (cynically, because they have to be extra health-conscious to justify screwing over players on salary), but some of the distancing changes are pretty dramatic for a plan that involves regular testing and other measures, and they're going to be extremely difficult to truly implement on a day-to-day basis, if not impossible.
