by TenuredVulture » Wed Aug 13, 2014 13:49:04

The other issue is that pretty much everything that could be done legislatively regarding civil rights has been done. And, to a large extent, this was wildly successful.

But legislation by itself can only do so much. Inequality in the application of the law is a significant problem. Killing black kids is illegal, but juries and judges look the other way all to often. Then there ways in which procedural equality do not amount to substantive equality. There's a big difference between saying Princeton does not discriminate on the basis of ethnicity and saying everyone has an equal opportunity to attend Princeton.

Which leads to economics. And in a real sense, just as civil rights victories were being achieved, economic mobility really slowed down, thus resulting in the persistence of severe economic inequality. And through much of the 70s and 80s, racial resentment was exploited to prevent this from happening, and that strategy has served economic elites quite well ever since.
