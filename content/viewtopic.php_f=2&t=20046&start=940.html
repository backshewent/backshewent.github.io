I'm not sure how deep in the weeds we want to get on this, but Will was surely aware of a movement in ethics called "virtue ethics" which is an attempt to rethink the Enlightenment idea of ethics that relied on reason to devise better and better principles--Kant would be an exemplar here--and go back to a more Aristotelian and Christian idea of ethics depending more on the idea of "wisdom". Rather than asking what the right principle is, under virtue ethics you consider what a virtuous person would do under the circumstances. That of course leads to the fundamental problem--who counts as a virtuous person? The virtues that Aristotle championed are different from the ones championed by say Christ. Benjamin Franklin presented yet another account.

Nevertheless, the primary strength is that it calls for discernment and judgment. We all know that truth telling better than lying. However, we also know that sometimes, lying is necessary. Moral wisdom allows us to recognize when we should make exceptions to the principle of telling the truth.

Alasdair MacIntyre's After Virtue is a key text here. I only mention it because in the intro, MacIntyre recasts A Canticle for Liebowitz (a book I recall some around here liking very much) as a tale about morality rather than science.
