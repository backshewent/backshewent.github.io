Note: The following is not to argue with or against any poster here, but just to add my perspective.

The nation-as-a-business analogy isn't perfect, but occasionally useful.

Companies borrow in order to grow. If the return on borrowed money is greater than the cost of the borrowed money, taking on debt makes sense for a company.

If the USA can stimulate sustainable GDP growth by borrowing, by growing the debt, it's fine until the growth of the debt outpaces the growth of GDP.

Of course, it's all easier to manage when interest rates are essentially zero.

You don't need a degree in economics to know that interest rates move in loooooong curves. They rise for 30 years, then fall for 30 years, with plenty of deceptive bumps within each broad move.

What is "normal" for interest rates? To me, it's 4 or 5% passbook savings and 6% mortgages, because that held sway in my youth. When I was 21, though, I earned 17% on my money market account. Now I earn 0.2%. What is normal?



Bottom line, it's hard to imagine that we aren't at the bottom, facing a 30 year period of rising rates (full disclosure, I've been thinking that for 10 years). And if we have to pay Chinese investors 5, 7, 9% to buy Treasury Bonds, then our enormous debt is gonna be an enormous problem.

And no one more to blame than Trump and his band of idiot GOP cowards in congress.

-----------------------------------------

Budget deficits matter.

A TRADE deficit, not so much. I never understood why we wring our hands over it. Rich nations buy from poorer ones.
