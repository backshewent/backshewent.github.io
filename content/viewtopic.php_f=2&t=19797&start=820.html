JFLNYC wrote:They know they can stop it but only if they’re willing to risk falling on their own swords.


Best case scenario--it's a collective action problem, and you simply need a few to take the leap and the rest will follow. In today's hyper partisan context though that's a bit like asking some NJ liberal to refrain from voting for Menendez--sure, I despise the man and I believe he's as corrupt as the day is long, but the alternative is another vote for Moscow Mitch and the Republican agenda.

But I don't believe that's really what's happening. It's important to keep in mind that Republicans are getting pretty much everything they really want from a Republican President with Trump--right wing judges, tax cuts, a license to destroy the environment for profit, and other deregulation, and continued growth in economic inequality. It's not just about them being afraid of their base.

But there are more troubling possibilities than partisanship and collective action reluctance. First, they might not care. We're still at a point where the basic problems of a Trump administration are rather abstract. I certainly believe his foreign policy is a total disaster, and it's doing long term harm to our national interests. But in terms of my day to day life, it's not making any real difference. If I'm a Republican Senator, my constituents aren't complaining, and it really doesn't matter anyway--none of this poses anything like an existential threat to the US. Even ISIS at its strongest can't really do anything much to us living here in the US.

Bush was a pretty terrible President, and the Iraq invasion was a miscalculation of horrendous proportions--at this point, in fact, I'd say it did more damage than anything Trump has done. But it took several palpable events for Bush's popularity to crash. Katrina, the Mortgage crisis, in addition to the realization that his Iraq policy was a costly failure. (You can add that his nomination of Harriet Miers and his attempt at immigration reform cost him support on his right flank and thus made them less vigorous in their defense of his other policy failures.)
