Unlike all other federal courts, the Supreme Court has discretion to decide which cases it will hear. The Supreme Court gets thousands of petitions for certiorari, but only issues a writ in a fraction of cases. The Court will only issue a writ if four of the nine Justices vote to do so. Justices usually take the importance of a given case and the need to issue a final decision before deciding to grant certiorari. If four Justices do not agree to grant certiorari, the petition is denied. If a case is "denied cert", the decision of the lower court is final.

If I understand correctly, states do early voting differently and report numbers differently. (Do mail in/absentee ballots count as early votes? I think it depends on the state.) For example, I've seen reports that in Texas, early votes account for 90% of the number of people who voted in total in 2016. That seems to suggest that in Texas at least, we're looking at very high participation rates, even given the fact that the population in Texas is increasing rapidly.

Keep in mind in some states, early voting ends as soon as Friday. Few, if any states, offer early voting on Monday. On the other hand, I've heard that on average, 4 million people nationally are voting per day.
