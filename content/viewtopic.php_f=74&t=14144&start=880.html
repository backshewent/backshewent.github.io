cshort wrote:Jim Henson and his family made millions off of characters he created and developed through Sesame Street. While the entities are non-profit, hard to argue that the creator didn't profit. Would seem to me PBS dropped the ball by not getting something from Henson (unless they were prohibited by law). Non-profits in other areas get royalties/fees from products where they provide the resources for people to invent or create, seems to me PBS could have done the same thing, seeing as they were the money making vehicle for Henson.


Many Muppets were created by Henson years in advance of the creation of Sesame Street and in the years following his departure. Children's Television Workshop (as Sesame Workshop was then known) asked Henson to join Sesame Street. Henson and CTW did, in fact, make a deal for the characters he developed for Sesame Street. The deal was that he would waive his performance fees on the show in exchange for owning the characters, AND that he would also split the revenues from the characters' exploitation. Sesame Workshop now owns the Sesame Street Muppet characters.

Rather than Henson exploiting Sesame Street (and, by extension PBS) to make his fortune, PBS called Henson "the spark that ignited our fledgling broadcast service." Sesame Workshop, in turn, receives only a fraction of its production costs from PBS. A portion of its costs are also coverd by its licensing of the characters created by Henson.

Overall then, it was a pretty good deal for all parties concerned. Jim Henson made a handsome return for his hard work, Sesame Workshop survives in significant part by virtue of his work and PBS (and, by extension all Americans) got a very good return on its investment: a public broadcasting service due in no small part to his efforts. It's worth noting that the arrangement is quite analogous to how commercial TV works, with the creator (e.g., the production company) profiting handsomely from a hit show, the studio (similar to Sesame Workshop) which initially financed the show also profiting and finally, the network which airs the show (like PBS) attracting viewers, which allows them to sell advertising and to profit thereby. It's also worth noting that, similar to PBS, typically commercial broadcast networks have not shared in the ownership of entertainment shows (e.g., The Big Bang Theory), although networks generally produce and own their own news, documentary and certain others shows.
