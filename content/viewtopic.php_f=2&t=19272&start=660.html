Short answer: Because when they granted Lord Baltimore and William Penn their respective colonies, people in England didn't have good maps of the area. (Delaware of course was originally part of Pennsylvania, but the Swedes living their didn't like being governed by a bunch of Quakers, so the three "lower counties" became their own colony. (Though still under the governorship of William Penn and in fact would have the same governor as Pennsylvania until 1776).

Here's an interesting bit of trivia--the contested border between Maryland and Pennsylvania was finally demarcated by the Mason-Dixon line--everyone knows that. But once the line reaches Delaware, it takes a 90 degree turn towards the south, goes to the bottom of Delaware, takes another 90 degree turn, and then continues east until it reaches the ocean. So, though Maryland is south of the Mason-Dixon line, Delaware is north of it.

More fun things about the shape of these states: the Twelve Mile Circle that defines the northern border of Delaware, and continues to have ramifications, largely because the Delaware River is not split in the middle with New Jersey, as would usually be the case, but is in some areas virtually part and parcel of Delaware.

Its existence dates back to 1681, when Charles II granted a deed to William Penn north of the already chartered Maryland. Charles created an exception, consisting of 12 miles around the town of New Castle and extending down the peninsula, as these lands were held by the Duke of York, who had won them in conquest from the Dutch colonists. Later, on August 24, 1682, the Duke granted these lands to Penn as well, giving him:

all that the Towne of Newcastle otherwise called Delaware and All that Tract of Land lying within the Compass or Circle of 12 Miles about the same scituate lying and being upon the River Delaware in America And all Islands in the same River Delaware and the said River and Soyle thereof lying North of the Southermost part of the said Circle of 12 Miles about the said Towne.
