drsmooth wrote:probably a contrarian view, but I feel like pretending that persuasion will change minds of people who made the appalling mistake of confusing drumpf's ravings with an expression of their own frustration with actual problems America faces, and so actively VOTED FOR HIM TO BE POTUS rather than just declaring they'd vote for mickey mouse vs that horrible awful woman person, is a formula for inaction.

He's spent far too much time doing it already. It needs to be done with.

You're right, in a way. You probably don't have the skills to persuade anyone to change their minds. The problem for the rest of us in the real world who have an ability to display empathy, engage with opposing arguments, and persuade open-minded people, is that we feel a moral obligation to act in a way that produces change. I'm not just talking about posting on an internet forum, although I find that my discourse online is correlated in tone with my discourse in person. Demonizing Trump, or making fun of his hair, or poking fun at Steve Bannon's skin complexion, is a good way to get political opponents to defend them, empathize with their shortcomings, and identify with them.

People don't like to be told that they're wrong, and doing that is a terrible way to persuade people. I guess it could work sometimes, but various psych studies have shown that people are not persuaded by evidence and facts that contradict their worldview. Our minds reflexively reject facts that contradict what we believe to be true, which is why facts are only marginally useful to crafting a winning argument about policy or strategy. People don't usually argue facts, and having the correct facts doesn't mean you win the argument. The facts have to be presented as somehow consistent with an aspect of a person's position, or aligned with an argument they've used in the past, and they have to have the time and room to re-evaluate their new position.

The global warming "debate" is a great example of a policy question that cannot be refuted by facts, but by reframing the debate in a way that allows people to consider why they believe what they believe.
