So what do my fellow PA residents feel about the "Marsy's Law" constitutional amendment ballot question in the November polls?

On its face, it seems OK. But the ACLU and others have spoken out against it, saying the question itself is unconstitutional- since the PA constitution requires each change to be voted on individually. The singe yes/no question encompasses changes to "three different articles, eight different sections, and one schedule to the Pennsylvania constitution”.

I'm sorta leaning "no" right now, solely on procedural reasons. This precedent could allow other questions in the future where the uninformed electorate could vote things in with trojan horses.

Generally, I favor the idea of ballot initiatives, but like anything else political, there are always going to be loopholes that can be exploited, and the system is open to potential abuses. But for issues such as legalization of marijuana and raising the minimum wage, it is a way for voters to to an end run around entrenched special interests that often thwart these kind of moves at the state legislature.

Of course, voters can do stupid stuff too. But if you spend any time paying attention to state legislatures, when it comes to stupid stuff, they exceed voter capacity.

The problem here has not been ballot initiatives, per se, which can be very useful. The bigger problem has been bundled initiatives, such as this one from last year:

Amendment 11, Property Rights; Removal of Obsolete Provision; Criminal Statutes, would revise the Constitution to remove some language, including a provision that stops “aliens ineligible for citizenship” from owning property and wording approving a high-speed rail system. It would also remove the state’s Savings Clause, which prohibits retroactively applying the amendment of a criminal statute to sentencing for a crime committed before the change, and clarify that repealing a criminal statute would not necessarily affect the prosecution of that crime committed previously.
