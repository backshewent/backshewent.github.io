by FTN » Tue Feb 14, 2012 13:46:17

But I dont think they will go over the luxury tax threshold. When the Red Sox were spending ridiculous sums of money on the draft every year, directly defying the Commish, the Phillies (who had similar revenues and the same 100% attendance) were more closely adhering to the Commish's recommendations. MLB doesn't want teams spending over the luxury tax, which is why the punishment for repeat offenders is a really high tax rate. Going back to Bill Giles, the Phillies have always kept in good standing with the office of MLB. They don't spend lavishly in Latin America or the draft, which pleases Selig. I just can't see the philosophy changing. The Phillies are in the financial elite. With the added revenue, I can see them trying to modify/improve CBP and give fans even more for their money. I can also see them freezing ticket prices or really slowing the increase. But I don't see them doing anything that would piss off Selig/the next Commissioner.

by FullCount » Tue Feb 14, 2012 15:04:46

It's important to note the Phillies also profit from TV advertising sales, which they still handle in-house, to avoid sponsor conflicts in ballpark signage. The amount the Phillies earn from those sales is unknown, but in any future mega-deal, the Phillies could choose to cede the revenue from TV ad sales to Comcast or another broadcaster, if the price is right.
I heard that they retained the right for ad sales before but had not seen this explanation. It appears to explain why they seem to have a very small inventory of broadcast advertisers whose commercials appear over and over and over. On the face of it, it's an unusual approach to have in-stadium signage drive TV ad deals but there should be added value for any advertiser who signs on for a sponsorship deal without fear of getting ambushed by a competitor buying up ad time. Presumably the Phils should be getting more than the signage and ad rates would each cost independently. But the limited inventory makes you wonder.
