by jerseyhoya » Thu Jun 27, 2019 18:45:22

Werthless wrote:Do political scientists have a solution to the problem of extreme gerrymandering, to the extent they see it as a problem?

I have a hard time wrapping my head around the argument that the federal courts do not have an interest in how Congressional maps can be drawn, where free and fair elections and access is clearly under the umbrella of federal oversight.

Kyle Kondik @kkondik
More Kyle Kondik Retweeted Nathan I Winston
is it possible to say that gerrymandering is both a hugely important and also a hugely overrated problem in politics? I freely admit to oscillating between those positions and holding both positions in my head at the same time.

Not that Kyle is a political scientist, but he plays one sometimes, and think that's a pretty concise summary. It matters in some outcomes, but its impact is often overrated. You could switch to a computer drawing districts tomorrow, and most districts would still be uncompetitive, polarization wouldn't be fixed, and many states would have delegations that are 'unfair' based on total votes. Every state adopting an independent redistricting commission that drew lines ignoring political data and where incumbents lived would make things better but wouldn't be a silver bullet to fix American politics.

And federal courts have an interest in how Congressional maps can be drawn. They require that districts are drawn to be of equal population, and they have intervened regularly for the past few decades on issues involving race. They haven't historically intervened on partisan gerrymanders, and today's decision just continues that.
