Pseudoscience ramble that's been bouncing in my brain so I need to get it out. I've been thinking about simulation theory since it's been popping up in some of the media I consume. I've seen a thought experiment being thrown out a lot as an argument for it that once a simulated universe is capable of creating its own simulations you get a sort of fractal tree where the only deciding factor is creators choosing not to create. This, it's argued, leads to an infinite number of branches from each root in the real universe making it almost statistically impossible for us to be in the real universe. I'm of two minds with this because I think it glosses over the computing power needed to simulate a universe and assumes Moore's Law will hold true indefinitely rendering it trivial, but I think that ignores the thermodynamic limitations of trying to move the required masses of information through even a subatomic structure in a quantum processor. And if there does exist any kind of limit on how many levels of simulation are possible from each root in the real universe then the probability would change from 1 out of an infinity to some usable chance. But, my brain yells at me, would the many worlds theory switch that shit around by having some universes never creating a simulation which would mean an infinite number without simulations...
