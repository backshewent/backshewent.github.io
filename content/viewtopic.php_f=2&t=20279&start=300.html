"any structure, plaque, statue or other monument on public property commemorating the service of any person or persons in the armed forces of the United States."


Ha ha! Great catch! One of the good things about being the winner of the conflict is we can change definitions to suit our needs. Somebody from the South might argue - not without merit - that what the Civil War established, legally, was that the "Confederate States of America" never existed as a legal entity because it was illegal for the states to secede, and so all military service to the several Southern states was in fact service in the armed forces of the United States.

Of course that last part is an absurdity because even disregarding the Confederacy as a legal entity, those Confederate armies were at best state militias, not armed forces of the federal entity, and they were in open rebellion against the federal entity.

The War enforced Lincoln's legal theory that membership in the Union was permanent and a state could not secede. The Emancipation Proclamation was issued on the notion that there was no Confederacy and Lincoln was still president of the rebelling states. On the other hand, when it came to the Fugitive Slave Act, after early moments where some Union generals obeyed the act and returned escaped slaves to the South (since the South in the Union theory was still part of the USA and therefore federal law was still controlling), General Benjamin Butler, early in the War, established what became the Union position on the matter, as in this exchange with Major John Baytop Cary of the Confederacy, recounted by Adam Goodheart:

Cary got down to business. “I am informed,” he said, “that three Negroes belonging to Colonel Mallory have escaped within your lines. I am Colonel Mallory’s agent and have charge of his property. What do you mean to do with those Negroes?”

“I intend to hold them,” Butler said.
“Do you mean, then, to set aside your constitutional obligation to return them?”

Even the dour Butler must have found it hard to suppress a smile. This was, of course, a question he had expected. And he had prepared what he thought was a fairly clever answer.

“I mean to take Virginia at her word,” he said. “I am under no constitutional obligations to a foreign country, which Virginia now claims to be.”

“But you say we cannot secede,” Cary retorted, “and so you cannot consistently detain the Negroes.”

“But you say you have seceded,” Butler said, “so you cannot consistently claim them. I shall hold these Negroes as contraband of war, since they are engaged in the construction of your battery and are claimed as your property.”
