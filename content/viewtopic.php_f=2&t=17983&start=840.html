Seems like an interesting question. It seems stupid to permit states to effectively decide for themselves how the manage the way their citizenry selects representatives to the federal government. And in fact they aren't permitted to do odious things that effectively tilt selection processes based on race, for example.

But there's no one right way to draw boundaries, eliminating politics from the process isn't possible, and the typical form of a court's ruling - "we aren't telling you how to do it, but you can't do it XYZ way" - seems likely only to create - or expand? - a "redistricting lobbying" industry.

So good, on balance, for your career prospects?

One of the most difficult things with redistricting is many of the nonpartisan, basic goals of drawing districts are in conflict.

-Communities of interest being grouped together
-Compactness
-Fair representation for racial and ethnic minorities
-A map that leads to a fair partisan balance based on the full state population
-Districts that are competitive in general elections

Tough to hit on more than 3 of 5 there, especially depending on the state, even if you're drawing districts with the best of intentions.

On balance I'd prefer 'independent' commissions'to draw district lines rather than letting legislators draw them for themselves. But independent commissions are still political. For example - How Democrats Fooled California's Redistricting Commission. It's tough to take politics out of politics.
