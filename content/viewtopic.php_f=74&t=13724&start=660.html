by dajafi » Tue Aug 07, 2012 23:52:46

I haven't been following this welfare issue closely. But from what I gather here and what I know about the policy and the labor market--which, not to be a schmuck, is basically what I thought about professionally for ten years, and avocationally when I wasn't on here or pp.com--I'm guessing it was a recognition that we have a structurally fucked up employment environment in which low-skilled people without work experience pretty much have zero chance of getting hired. (Actually, the only people who have a decent chance of getting hired right now are those already working. This is a problem, by the way, that tax policy won't solve.)

When faced with this problem--people having to do "work activities" to receive their public assistance when there's no chance of those work activities leading them to employment--a jurisdiction can make one of three choices: hire them to do presumably useful things, push them toward training and education that presumably will boost their employability if and when hiring picks up, or tell them to keep running on that treadmill of applying for jobs that they will never, ever get hired for. To be fair, Option 1 isn't "politically viable" because everyone knows that gummit employment is evil and useless. Option 2, the one I always liked as a believer that human capital is pretty much the best investment a society can make, is probably what the administration is holding out as a response to the fucked hiring environment. But Option 3, which maximizes the unpleasantness of the experience for public assistance recipients and communicates the pointlessness of their experience, is probably the most politically palatable.

My suspicion is that the Ayn Rand fetishists would prefer an Option 4, in which we kill the poor and sell their organs as spares to the "job creators." But we're probably a few years from anyone feeling comfortable enough to say that out loud.

Anyway, it seems pretty much everyone is okay with "their side" spinning like mad and stretching the truth beyond recognition. I'm not immune to this myself--I see Harry Reid talking about Romney's taxes and I simultaneously think he's a scumbag and feel some satisfaction that the Democrats now play more or less as rough as the Republicans. But mostly I ask myself how a system built on collaboration and compromise can function when the two parties are completely irreconcilable.
