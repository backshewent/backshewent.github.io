by dajafi » Sat Oct 06, 2012 12:44:24

My read is that the debate certainly made things closer, but 1) the jobs report probably blunted half of the Mitt-mentum; 2) the debates to come will intensify or dispel that trend (meaning that Romney basically got himself an opportunity to make major gains, rather than the gains themselves); and 3) Obama still probably has the better ground game. The president might have put it away Wednesday, and clearly did not.

Or you can think of it this way: Obama remains very likely to hold every state won by Kerry in 2004, plus New Mexico, where Silver has him at 97 percent likely to win, plus Ohio where Silver has him as an 85 percent favorite to win. If he has that, I think he can lose Nevada (where Silver still has him at 85 percent), Virginia (76 percent), Colorado (75 percent), Iowa (75 percent) and Florida (66 percent) and win re-election. The electoral college is just tough for Romney now.

The jobs report was huge. That the unemployment rate is now back to where it was when he took office is, as the vice-president might say, a big fucking deal. The Romney people keep trying to compare Obama to Carter in 1980, but the trend on the unemployment rate looks more like Reagan in 1984 (when he also got pummeled by the challenger in the first debate, probably worse than Obama did on Wednesday--people thought Reagan might be going senile).

If Romney beats him badly again in the town hall debate and there's some external bad news for the administration, he might get in position to win. But we're not there yet.
