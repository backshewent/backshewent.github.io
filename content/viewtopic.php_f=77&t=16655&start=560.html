The state is concerned with the desires of the majority. Libertarians care about the rights of the individual. Those two things aren't the same.

mozartpc27 wrote: If an individual wants to do something society has deemed undesirable, and has therefore elected representatives to enact laws to identify and punish this undesirable behavior (a collection of practices we refer to as "the state"), then the individual must have his rights curbed because his/her freedom has encroached on everyone else's, by definition.

I think the problem with libertarian thought is present in this contradiction - that it can simultaneously suggest an unlimited space for individual action, and the permanent primacy of the state.

"everyone else's" refers to everyone else's desires, not rights. Libertarians don't believe in the right to have your desires imposed on others.
