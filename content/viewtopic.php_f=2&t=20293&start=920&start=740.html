What kind of trials are you talking about? Have you looked?

A. Studies on the types of masks, and their ability to filter particles: https://www.medrxiv.org/content/10.1101 ... 2.full.pdf
B. Studies that compare coronoviruses with other respiratory particles, such as those that cause influenza? https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-020-0843-2#Sec3
C. Studies of the geographic differences between areas with and without mask mandates? https://www.cnn.com/2020/06/30/business ... index.html

Now, if you are asking whether there is a controlled trial where scientists exposed a population to coronavirus while wearing masks, and had a similar population where a population was exposed while not wearing a mask, then that study does not exist. Hopefully it is clear why that study will never exist! You may need to adjust your expectations of evidence on this topic if that is your current standard. My suggestion is to think about what evidence for mask efficacy would look like, and I'd be happy to help you find a study that addresses it. For instance, is your question one of the following?

1. What is the estimated population-wide impact on coronovirus case rates where there are mandatory masks in public?
2. What is the impact of mask wearing on particle transfer?

There is a huge volume of studies on mask types, particle types, etc if you look for it. There is also wide consensus among epidemiologists who have studied the issue for more than just a few months, like you and I have. I would ask you to differentiate between magnitude uncertainty ("What is the magnitude of the decrease in spread does mask wearing produce?") versus effectiveness uncertainty ("Does mask wearing cause greater spread or lesser spread of coronavirus?") Here is a literature review I posted a few week ago:

http://files.fast.ai/papers/masks_lit_review.pdf

We all know that each and every preventative, therapeutic and vaccine will not be 100% effective on COVID, right? Just as they're not 100% effective with other diseases and ailments.

So why are the particular inadequacies of masks being highlighted?

Masks are one part (along with washing hands, disinfecting, social distancing, etc.) of the front line battle against this particular disease. In any battle, no single part of your arsenal is likely to be 100% effective. In a war since some soldiers are killed, planes shot down and some artillery misses its mark, do we throw up our hands and say: "No more infantry, planes or artillery until studies show they are 100% effective?" Likewise shall we give up on hospital personnel wearing gloves and surgeons scrubbing in because they're no guarantee against the transmission of disease?

Frankly, the whole discussion is nothing more than a misguided distraction which serves no useful purpose. If we believe we should employ only those preventative, therapeutic and vaccine methods which are proven 100% effective, we may as well just throw the sick out on the street and save ourselves a ton of healthcare costs.
