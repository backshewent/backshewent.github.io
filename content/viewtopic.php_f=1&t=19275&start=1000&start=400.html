I am sure somewhere there is a study on the psychology of prices driven by bidding - I.e., auction, which is essentially what this is - which would answer this question but I think it goes something like this:

If you want to win an item on eBay and the most you are willing to bid is $100, you are a fool to put in your max bid more than 15 seconds before the close of the auction, because even though eBay will only show your current bid (dictated by the amount of the next highest bid), not your absolute maximum bid, if you put your max bid in six days before the end of the auction you give everyone else time to talk themselves into $101. I myself have many times bid what I thought would be my max a minute or two before close, saw that I wasn’t the high bidder, thought to myself, “well, in for a penny, in for a pound,” and bumped my max bid by a few more dollars and wound up winning. Because I had time to mull it over.

The more time the Dodgers have to figure out accounting tricks, the more time the Giants have to contemplate the signing’s effect on ticket sales, the more likely it becomes they match or exceed the Phillies offer. If the Phillies have the high offer, and pull it, you do back to Boras and Harper (use yourself to drive down his price) what they are pretty clearly trying to do to you (use you to drive up his price to his preferred destination), so you get some measure of petty revenge.

And who doesn’t like petty revenge?

Here's how I think this played out.

Boras told LA and SF at the start that Harper only wants to play for them. He says we can leverage Philly because they have an owner who can't keep his mouth shut about spending money, so let them set the market, and once I get a firm offer from them, I'll come back to you and we can start the serious negotiations.

Meanwhile, Boras uses the whores in the baseball press like a fiddle, giving out small indications of info without giving away the real game, which is how to maximize SF and LA. So he uses the White Sox and Nats at first, they fall out, he lets the Phils squirm for a while, then I think he gets the ultimatum from Middleton last week.

That's when he makes the SF and LA news public, sure the Phils can see themselves as a contender, but Boras has already achieved what he wanted - to ensure his client plays where he wants, cranked up the AAV and over all price to set the market between SF and LA. He can always go back to Philly because they have a GM who a quant and blabbed about overvaluation with Machado and an owner who is going to lose significant credibility with his fan base...they can be counted on to do what needs to be done, useful idiots as Lenin said.

We aren't getting Harper and we never were. This was the long scam between Boras, Harper, and the two West Coast teams.

It's under 1% he's coming here. And it's always been that way.

For Middleton and Klentak to set up this scenario as the primary and secondary courses of action is mind blowingly stupid. Sure, they've added some real nice pieces, but they aren't going to the playoffs this year minus a small miracle. The real question is - does the Boras model get adopted by the rest of the league with the Phillies and free agents? I'd give it about 24-36 months to determine the impact on future free agents and their desire to play in Philly.


The Phils are already fighting against the glamour teams of SoCal, the glitz of NYC, the weird Boston fetish that somehow has emerged over the last two decades, and even the Texan big market/no state income tax teams.


The Phils better find another way to sell the team, the city, the area, because we might be seeing a slow semi-permanent fossilization as the best second tier team in the country (along with the Cubs).
