CalvinBall wrote:Moma can you give me photo lessons?


I'm a terrible teacher and this post may end up all over the place.

This is where I started ... Scott Kelby's books and blogs are great.

http://www.amazon.com/The-Digital-Photo ... 0321934946

Other than that, it's a lot of trial and error. I spent a whole day shooting in Arches. I didn't realize how badly I screwed up a lot of shots until about an hour before sunset. Speaking of, best landscape shots come within an hour of sunrise/sunset. Light is very important for landscape shots. The light on the horizon needs to be perfect. You won't get anything close to those colors any other time of the day. I think sunrise is better though, especially on damp mornings. The dampness reflects light better.

I still mainly stick to aperture priority and knowing the ISO limits of my gear. On top of that you really need the eye and figure out your post processing techniques. You can do a lot with Lightroom but you don't want to do too much. My post processing is down to maybe a minute a photo. Every now and then I find one that I like to play with though.

Look for angles you wouldn't normally expect from what your shooting.

Some examples:

Sunset, the stolen photo. Pretty close to sunset, so you get richer colors and much less white light. If I were to take this at noon, it'd be a boring, colorless photo.

Screwing up aperture. This photo should be great, but it was shot at like f/4 ... Should have been f/22 or at least about f/12. Such a flat image. No texture whatsoever. You can't make out the people:


By the time I realized I totally screwed my settings it was almost dark, but this one came out decent:


Also, the zoo is always a great place to play around with shots.


My mistake in Arches is that I left that Kelby book back at the campsite. To this day, I still carry it with me on travel.
