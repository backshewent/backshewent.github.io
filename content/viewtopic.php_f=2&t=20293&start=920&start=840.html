Our districts (regional middle/high school and elementary = two different districts) released a similar coordinated plan last week -- coordinated because the regional middle/high school district has 4-6 sending districts, and it appears they are also adopting the same plan. Students split into two cohorts alphabetically. All kids split going two days a week (either MT or ThF), with three days remote learning. Elementary kids going half-days, middle/high school starting two hours later and coming home 30 minutes later than "usual", but overall their day is shortened as well. They rarely do half days as policy, usually 1 or 2 a year. Special ed and ESL students go all 4 days, and you can go all remote if you choose, although the HS/Middle school is selling that option harder than the elementary school is.

It's masks all the time. The elementary kids might have snack in the classroom, but not lunch. The middle/high school hasn't addressed food yet, but they might also skip it based on the shortened day. The elementary school is going to be keeping kids in their classroom.

Both districts are now gathering questions, and have acknowledged they still need to come up with plans for daily screenings, outbreaks, etc.

It's been my feeling all along that transportation is going to be a huge factor in the decisions that would be made, and I see that in how they've come up with this plan. It's shorter round trip rides for the elemntary schools, so if the elementary kids are off-loaded by 8:30 (when school starts), the bus drivers have an hour to clean the bus and then get to the bus stop that is furthest away from the high school, and then get the kids to the high school. I say this knowing that we are at one of the furthest "corners" of the regional district, and it takes me 40-45 round trip to drop a kid off there and get back home.

We haven't been giving any time frames of opt-in or opt-out. Right now, our plan is sending the younger two to school and the oldest is going virtual. I will probably start out driving them, unless the middle/high school discourages it (which they normally do because of traffic issues). We worked out all the virtual learning issues with regard to technology and personal space, etc., and the kids actually did better with the virtual learning than in person. I was satisfied/happy with how the virtual work was handled for both districts. I'm not going to say it was easy, because sure as heck wasn't.

Reason for splitting the kids -- health concerns for the oldest. He falls into at least one of the higher-risk categories, and was very sick with respiratory illnesses last year (even hospitalized once). He's also a senior, and has a very light schedule this year. I don't see the point in sending him if he's going to be in 2-3 study halls. I want the other two to get a chance to know their teachers, especially if there's another wave of infections and schools go all virtual again.

Right now, I am optimistic that it will be "safe" to start school. I check the county stats daily, which give the age and town of each patient, and while I don't have percentages, the number of cases so far affecting school-aged is less than 20 for nearly 3000 total cases, and the majority of those cases are not in our district.


Why not split the kids geographically instead of alphabetically? Seems it would make for more efficient bus routes.

We don't have any busing in the district, so at least that headache is removed. Except of course for sports and extra-curriculars, which I'm still coaching as if we'll have, but figuring we will not.

by Rockinghorse » Thu Jul 30, 2020 07:11:22

RE: School plans, my wife teaches at a charter in the city

They floated a hybrid that had kids coming two days per week in "cohorts". The kids would stay in the same room all day while teachers moved around occasionally. They were only expecting around half to come back (virtual is always an option) so distancing in the room would be achievable. Everybody (teachers + parents) had lots of concerns with that so they moved to a hybrid of only one day per week for kids, mainly focused on helping them with tech issues and mental health stuff...so the in-person day wasn't really lessons, it was more emotional.

People rebelled again, and they are probably going all virtual now. They issued three criteria that have to be met by the city before they can go back in person, and as of now none of the three are met (below 5 percent infection rate, city can't be "high risk" level, and another test based on case count). Pretty unlikely that will change before the decision date on Aug. 7.

The general theme of the opposition was there are too many unknowns. So many things could go wrong, virus-related or otherwise, that creating contingency plans for everything was fruitless. Conversely, if they know it's all virtual, they can build on learnings from the spring and really focus on education rather than 1-2 days of child care. Everyone feels for the parents, but that was probably the end point anyway.
