It's more about OBP, OPS, and defensive position than race: OBP's: 410, 380, 397, 387. Jimmy was my favorite player, but Chase made outs in way fewer of his ABs than the other guys. It always seemed he'd find a way to get on or knock guys in because he had way fewer bad ABs than Jimmy or Ryan. The baseball world was very post-Moneyball then, and lots of fans really appreciated the guy who produced the fewest outs.

Chase's OPS+ was higher than Howard's over that period of time and he played excellent defense at a crucial defensive position, while Howard was below average at the easiest defensive position. And Rollins was just as good defensively, but his offensive numbers were 20% below Chase's.

Love Jimmy, but Chase deserves the recognition.

A few other factors I haven't seen mentioned yet:

- Rollins' "frontrunner" comment in August 2008 rubbed a lot of folks the wrong way. (EDIT:) Hamels wasn't / isn't necessarily nearly as beloved as he should be, IMO, from his 2009 WS comments either.

- The x times Rollins was benched for not hustling - which as jamie points out above is particularly important in Philadelphia, as we all know.

- Howard's decline (injury-related, shift-related, all of it) coinciding with his extension rubbed a lot of people the wrong way. The extension was still the right move, especially the term of it, but all people saw was a guy making $25m with declining production. Also think the above point about "playing the game the right way" hurt Howard in the sense of his (perceived) refusal to adjust his plate approach.
