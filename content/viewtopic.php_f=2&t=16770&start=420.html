by WheelsFellOff » Thu Oct 05, 2017 11:29:40

Have you read Neal Stephenson's work? He has a knack for extrapolating the effects of a technology to reshape a culture and illustrates it through his characters. Snow Crash is great and was recently optioned by Amazon to make a series based off it (along with Ringworld for any Larry Niven fans). It is a fresher take on cyberpunk than the 80's built around the internet and megacorporations. I also really liked Diamond Age's portrayal of nanotech not leading to a post-scarcity but further sharpening the divide of inequality.

China Mieville sticks out in my head as well from reading The Scar several years back. It shifts more towards fantasy than sci-fi, but not in a Tolkien/D&D way, more like how the Myst video games were.

by TenuredVulture » Thu Oct 05, 2017 13:06:23

Werthless wrote:Just read Dune and Replay. I'm pretty intrigued by this sub-genre of sci-fi, where the focus is less on technology/robots/etc and more with people. Anybody have any recommendations for similarly themed books?


If you want to go really old school, some of Ursula Leguin's sci fi stuff might fit. I think Lathe of Heaven is a near genius work. Her other sci-fi books (The Dispossessed, Left Hand of Darkness) might be along the lines of what you're looking for. Curiously, though most of her work takes a left-wing view of things, one of her short stories ("The Ones who Walk Away from Omelas") is often cited by libertarians as an argument against utilitarianism, which it is, but LeGuin should not be considered a libertarian. (She does however sometimes considers herself an anarchist.)
