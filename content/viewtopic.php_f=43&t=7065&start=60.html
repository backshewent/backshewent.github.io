by swishnicholson » Thu Nov 06, 2008 01:59:34

I sympathize with the parent with the eight year old next to him/her in the stadium or watching tv who was probably saying, "what did he say, what did he say?" or, worse, repeating it.

But I also think you have to look at the effect of the language.I hate casual profanity, which is usually used out of laziness and takes words that could be powerful, and often remain powerfully offensive to those to whom they can only denote their literal meaning, to convey the most banal and ill-considered thoughts. It's like using a shoe to try to drive a nail, because you can't be bothered to look in the basement for where you left your hammer. It doesn't do a very good job and wears out the shoe pretty quickly besides.

But profanity when used as an expletive can add powerful emphasis. and the reaction to Utley's use was mirth, approval and a powerful emotional release. It's hard to imagine other language he could have used to provoke this, and this is just the kind of reaction you want to provoke in a situation such as this, where people have gathered to celebrate and want to feel like the players are sharing similar emotions with them in regards to the the team's accomplishment.

So, by definition, Utley's use of the F word was "appropriate"-it conveyed well the emotion he was feeling to a crowd that wished to share in it.

Which is not to say that provoking middle schoolers to chant it in the halls is not an unfortunate side effect.
