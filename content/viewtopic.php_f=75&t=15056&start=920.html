Some guesses on why the obamacare website is a steaming pile of poop that cost more than LinkedIn...

1) The US government's internet infrastructure is quite antiquated. The infrastructure is probably not a whole lot newer than when the Social Security, IRS, etc. websites initially launched.

2) There's probably only a handful of web designers directly employed by the federal government, definately not enough for such a herculean project as healthcare.gov. So the federal government contracts out most web design projects to government contractors who then subcontract out the actual work. This complicates things even more and is not conducive to such tech projects. You end up with the top "tier" of contractors enriched by a half billion dollars while delivering a massive clusterfuck of 404 pages.

Frankly, I think we need to go further than eliminating only the ACA because of the website problems. I think we need to expand the idea by requiring any enterprise with website troubles to close. For example, for years the MLB.tv website has been a nightmare to navigate, so MLB is, therefore, a failure and should be required to close. I know lots of people who have trouble with the Amazon.com website, so let's get rid of that faulty business, too. I don't know anyone who has successfully been able to use the websites for Aetna or Blue Cross/Blue Shield. Why are they being allowed to continue to sell health insurance?

In arguing against the expansion of Medicaid in Virginia, the Koch Kadets point to a University of Virginia study that found worse surgical outcomes for Medicaid patients than for the uninsured and, therefore, contend that Medicaid coverage is worse than no coverage at all. It's better for Virginia's 400,000 uninsured to remain uninsured than to have Medicaid coverage.

I think they're onto something.

Think of the savings if we were to eliminate health insurance altogether? People will encouraged to take more responsibility for their own health rather than frittering away money needlessly on doctor and hospital visits. Break an arm? Rub some dirt on it! Got cancer? It's your unhealthy lifestyle!

As Eric Cantor argued during the shutdown, we just want the fairness of the same rules applying to everyone. What would level the playing field more than no one having health insurance?
