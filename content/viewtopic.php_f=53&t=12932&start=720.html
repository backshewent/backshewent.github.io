Barry Jive wrote:it's literally impossible to measure an intangible. "measurable" is an antonym of "intangible"


Intangible literally means you can't touch it. It is more generally used to mean things you can't perceive directly with your senses. But you can measure things you can't perceive with your senses indirectly. It might be harder and there may be more validity and reliability problems, but you can do it. Things like consumer confidence or approval ratings of public officials can't be touched or sensed tangibly, but they can be and are measured all the time.

Swiggers wrote:FFS, "it can't be proven statistically" is not the same thing as "it doesn't exist." This is the biggest problem with most Sabermetric-oriented writers. They think it IS the same thing.


I think the operable thought chain is that if these alleged intangibles don't provide a measurable effect, then they are worthless and usually aren't worthy of the connotation in which they are used. After all, it's the results that matter in professional baseball.

Not that there are non-performance areas that aren't important, though. If having a guy on your team makes other guys sign more autographs or visit more kids in hospitals, that's a good thing. But intangibles are always spoken of in a way that indicates they make the team perform better on the field.
