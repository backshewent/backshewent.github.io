I understand where you're coming from but I have to disagree.

I fear that, from a legal standpoint, he and the other justices will view this as less of a Constitutional issue and more of a Congressional screwup. Although the Constitution gives Congress the power of the purse there is the argument that Congress ceded some of that power to the President in the National Emergencies Act. Congress gave themselves certain checks and balances in that Congress can undo a state of emergency declaration with either a joint resolution and the President's signature, or with a veto-proof majority vote. However, in this case it's likely that neither of those will occur. In such a case I think it's possible -- perhaps likely -- that SCOTUS will defer to Congress' wishes (and the President's discretion) as set forth in the NEA and as confirmed by their not overriding the President's declaration. Essentially they'll be saying to Congress: "You created this situation by ceding some of your power to the President in the National Emergencies Act. If you now don't like the outcome, change the law."
