It's Sunday in Germany, which means it's Election Day there.

There've been some significant countries holding elections in 2013 (Israel, Australia, Iran, Venezuela, Italy, etc.), but Germany is probably the biggest country in terms of global significance. All opinion polls show that Merkel's CDU/CSU center right alliance is expected to get the largest share of the votes, but beyond that, uncertainty reigns. The CDU won't have an outright majority of seats, so they'll need a coalition partner. Their current coalition partner, the free market FDU, might not hit 5%, and even if they do they might not be able to get Merkel to a majority. That leaves the most likely outcome a reprisal of the 2005-09 grand coalition with the Christian Democrats governing with their main rivals, the SPD (Social Democrats, center left).

The other big wild card is how the AfD, a party devoted to getting Germany out of the Euro, will perform. If they get into parliament, it would be a big break with post war German politics where there's mostly been a consensus on European issues.

There is a chance left wing parties (SPD, the Greens and the Left Party) will have a majority of seats, but even if this happens, it's likely Merkel will remain chancellor (with the SPD as partners) because all parties have traditionally refused to invite the Left Party into a coalition as they're the successors to the old East German Communist Party.

Der Spiegel has some nice English language coverage directed at people who don't know much about German politics. They've promised coverage here tomorrow starting at 11 AM tomorrow. Two of the best and most basic articles being a write up on each party competing and a look at how the voting system works.
