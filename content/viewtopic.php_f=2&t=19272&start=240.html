Not because they're more moral, but because preferred spots to student athlete recruits is very limited, and it would have to be truly worthwhile to sabotage your program by wasting one on a student unlikely to contribute. Jerome Allen made out pretty well, but was it really worth it when he was forced to leave Penn after a few years becasue he wasn't winning enough, a fact that certainly was not helped by giving a spot to a guy who never saw the court.


I would guess this depends on how much the school cares about winning in the sport in question. USC clearly cares about winning in track, and Penn at least used to care about winning in basketball. But there's probably not a whole lot of pressure to win in most non-revenue sports programs. And even D-1 coaches in these sports have few if any scholarship athletes in their programs. If a coach isn't holding a scholarship over a student's head, there's really nothing he or she can do to require the athlete to perform. Successful coaches in these programs probably have ways to figure out how serious an athlete is about continuing at the college level, so it's not difficult to see how this could go on. And frankly, if there is pressure to win, a coach might figure that since their job is so insecure as it is, they might as well take whatever they can get while they can.
