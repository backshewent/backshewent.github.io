it appears, but somewhat strained hypothesis, that some portion of the population has some immunity to begin with from exposure to various virus' over our lifetimes. this isn't that much of an absurd statement and the article goes into pre-existing immunity to the 2009 H1N1 outbreak.

there's been two thoughts on why asia pacific hasn't had the virus issues that the rest of the world seemingly has
-mask culture
-pre-immunity to being in a hot spot of coronavirus'

study above says that in singapore that 50% of the blood donors tested had t-cell resistance.

at an innate 50% immunity the virus would really struggle to spread.

here's someone spin on London's exposure and a R(t) of the virus
https://pbs.twimg.com/media/EcO1ULiXkAE ... name=large


that article is not a study itself, it is a summary of various studies. total of 5 studies, 4 of which are in pre-print, meaning they have not been fully reviewed.

Taken together, five studies report evidence of pre-existing T cells that recognize SARS-CoV-2 in a significant fraction of people from diverse geographical locations.


this does not mean those people will have "immunity," it means their t-calles recognized this virus. that's it. what response it triggers in people is yet to be studied. even if your t-cells recognize the virus you are still likely going to get sick.

in the singapore study they took 18 people with no history of SARS, COVID-19 or contact with SARS/COVID-19 patients, and found that 9 of them "possess T cells targeting the ORF-1 coded proteins NSP7 and 13, which were rarely detected in COVID-19- and SARS-recovered patients." i would NOT extrapolate that mean either
1) 50% of the general population would show the same or
2) this means they have "t-cell resistance" (ie immunity) to the actual virus. and again, this was a sample size of 18 people.

the nature article concludes:
"In conclusion, it is now established that SARS-CoV-2 pre-existing immune reactivity exists to some degree in the general population."


immune reactivity =/= immunity
