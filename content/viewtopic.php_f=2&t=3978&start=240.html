I bought a HP TouchPad as part of a laptop/TouchPad bundle at Best Buy back in early November. I like the TouchPad and webOS. It's too bad most webOS devices (i.e. pretty much all the smartphones except the never commercially released HP Pre 3) have crappy hardware.

As for other bitchin' gadgets, I nominate:

1) ASUS Transformer Prime - this quad-core tablet with detachable keyboard dock (making it a Android netbook computer) points toward the future of large tablets/netbooks/small ultrabooks IMO and builds upon the success of the original ASUS Transformer (which was frequently out-of-stock for a few months after it was released). Even though I tend to be anti-Apple due to their desire to want you to do things their way and only their way, I'd LOVE if Apple came out with something similar (i.e. integrated but detachable keyboard dock) with the iPad 3; I'd seriously consider picking one up if it was a reasonable price.

2) Pretty much any Windows Phone OS device - seriously, regardless if you use an Android, an iPhone, a Blackberry, or something else, you'll probably be impressed by the smoothness, live tile content, and overall user interface/experience if you play around with a Windows Phone. The Metro UI and WP OS is very different than the Windows OS on a PC (or for that matter, Windows Mobile, at least based on what I've read).

Not really a tech advancement; it's bigger than the regular RAZR to accommodate a bigger battery.

One of the neat things that's starting to be implemented in phones is NFC (Near Field Communication) which allows two phones, or a phone and another device, that are very close to one another (like, so close they're touching) to exchange data. There isn't much that uses this yet, but one of the things expected is a sort of virtual wallet - so you could just tap your smartphone on the little gadget at the register like you can today with certain debit cards. There are all sorts of other possibilities for NFC as well. I'd look for a phone that has an NFC chip in it.

The difference between smart phones and smart cards is that there's no way to control the latter - the chip in the card can at least theoretically be accessed by anything in range. But in a smart phone the NFC chip can be turned off through software; e.g. unless you're in an app that uses NFC, the chip isn't even on.
