I'm going to caveat us this entire post as I am recovering from jaw surgery at the moment and might come off overly snippy. I apologize in advance.

It's not hard for them to find proper care. Military personnel have tons of options for the treatment of PTSD. I have a slight touch of it, it's manageable with a lot of physical exercise and some reflective hobbies like fishing. A guy I work with has a very bad case of it, with traumatic brain injury (concussion) on top of it. He has at least 12-15 hours of therapy a week at various doctors and therapists in the DC area. He is by no means an exception - military personnel and veterans have wide ranging options on treating PTSD.

I see two problems at the moment with the treatment of PTSD and the perception of PTSD.

1. We're dealing with PTSD seriously for the first time in our nation's history. It almost became a qualifier for a Purple Heart (and should be). This means we're on the cutting edge on understanding the causes, effects, and treatments of PTSD. To make matters worse, you are also dealing with TBI - Traumatic Brain Injury - in many cases, and that can amplify existing effects or have unique effects that make diagnosis and treatment difficult. In addition, PTSD, like most psychological damage, is not something that can be cured and short term alleviation is rare.

2. The second issue is that many vets are not aware of all the options available to them, and in many cases, they aren't even aware that they are suffering from something...there's a lot of pride associated with these folks, and to many it is admitting that have something wrong with them. Psychological illnesses are like that...many people don't admit they are depressed, etc...

The perception of lack of treatment is out there because the VA is overwhelmed, the strategic communications from the military could be a LOT better, and we're not really sure how to deal with this at the moment...we're learning as we go...not an excuse not to take care of our own, we have to do better, but we're really treating PTSD with the seriousness it requires for the first time in the history of the nation.

See you tomorrow, feeling pretty shitty right now.
