thephan wrote:
The more the rural U.S. struggles, company officials said, the more places Dollar General has found to prosper. “The economy is continuing to create more of our core customer,” Chief Executive Todd Vasos said in an interview at the company’s Goodlettsville, Tenn., headquarters.

“We are putting stores today [in areas] that perhaps five years ago were just on the cusp of probably not being our demographic,” he said, “and it has now turned to being our demographic.”


Maybe DJT can be CiC of Dollar General.


There's no doubt that DG is putting stores in places where they didn't exist five years ago--my struggling rural county has seen at least three new ones in the last couple of years.

But at least two of these areas really didn't have any store at all five years ago, and the other is a slightly larger town that now has two stores. I would guess that while the people who use the DG in these places are low-middle income, their motivation for using DG isn't because they can't afford to shop at Wal-Mart anymore, but because they can get a gallon of milk or a box of cereal at a low price without a 45 minute round trip.

Now, there are probably other places in rural America where DG really is building because people can't afford Kroger or Wal-Mart anymore. (And the big difference isn't that DG has lower prices, but rather that you can buy in smaller quantity, thus if you're strapped for cash, you can get a small box of cereal at DG while Wal-Mart only has the larger sizes.)

Anyway, my point is that at least in part, DG's growth comes from putting stores in areas that didn't have anything before--it's function is more like a convenience store than a cheap store.
