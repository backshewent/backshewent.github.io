Bucky wrote:didn't i read something here that antibodies aren't the only factor in immunity? something like once your immune system knows how to make the antibodies it will when necessary?


viewtopic.php_f=2&t=20281&p=3030596&hilit=antibodies+facebook#p3030596

I'm seeing people start to share this, assuming after reading it that people won't have long-lasting immunity to COVID-19. As someone with a PhD in microbiology/immunology, this kind of makes me say, "so what?"

Here's why: Antibody titers naturally wane after infection (or immunization), often to very low or undetectable levels. And for good reason: your immune system is too busy to spend energy cranking out the same antibodies if there's no virus around. It's got other pathogens to fight! However, the immune system remembers that it once made those antibodies (the cells that are responsible for this are called memory B cells). If you're exposed to that virus again, your memory B cells wake up and start producing antibodies again within a few days. (See figure from WHO Vaccine Immunology in the comments... this applies to natural infection as well.) This is just how the immune system works. There's no reason to believe that COVID-19 immunity would be any different.

Maybe Reuters (and other news outlets, for that matter) should employ some medical writers who actually understand how the immune system works. Until they do, they're unnecessarily causing fear and panic in the general public, most of whom haven't sat through hundreds of hours of immunology lectures!

TenuredVulture wrote:Has there been a single documented case of reinfection other than what look most likely to be based on false positives?



There's a post on TPM behind their firewall today about this. It's anecdotal, but some instances are being reported. For example, some doctor in NJ says he's had two cases....

But these more recent reports sound more rigorous and detailed. I recently saw a report from a respected physician (the Vox author notes this report too) in New Jersey who said he’d found two cases of reinfection. In one case there was an initial positive test, a clearing of the disease confirmed by a negative test, a positive antibodies test and then reinfection after a loss of antibodies. The additional serology evidence makes this case much harder to explain away.

I don’t think we can consider this established. The multiple of anecdotes isn’t data. There’s a reason we base such findings on systematic research rather than clinicians findings. But we should also listen closely to the latter because that is likely where we’ll first hear about new information out of sync with conventional or established thinking.


This will need a lot of further research, obviously. I could see this disease getting in damaged tissues and hiding there until it reemerges, so a patient was never really rid of it.

This was also terrifying....

Here is a very concerning article just published by a practicing physician in Vox. He reports, admittedly on very limited clinical evidence and anecdotal reports from colleagues, that people appear able to get reinfected with COVID after fully clearing an initial infection. He also suggests that such reinfections may be more severe. He compares it to diseases like dengue fever where you get a worse case each time you get it.


If this is the case, getting a vaccine could open you up to a much more severe infection, which is the opposite of what you would want from a vaccine.

https://www.vox.com/2020/7/12/21321653/ ... d-immunity

In my opinion, my patient’s experience serves as a warning sign on several fronts.

First, the trajectory of a moderate initial infection followed by a severe reinfection suggests that this novel coronavirus might share some tendencies of other viruses such as dengue fever, where you can suffer more severe illness each time you contract the disease.

Second, despite scientific hopes for either antibody-mediated or cellular immunity, the severity of my patient’s second bout with Covid-19 suggests that such responses may not be as robust as we hope.

Third, many people may let their guard down after being infected because they believe they are either immune or incapable of contributing to community spread. As my patient’s case demonstrates, these assumptions risk both their own health and the health of those near them.


Last, if reinfection is possible on such a short timeline, there are implications for the efficacy and durability of vaccines developed to fight the disease.
