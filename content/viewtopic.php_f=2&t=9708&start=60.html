I forget if I told this story or not, but when I was in Vegas in June, I went to Cirque du Soleil with a handful of my friends because tickets to the Love show came in our travel package. I was stoked. After trying my ass off and coming close but ultimately failing to acquire the proper medical supplies to prepare me suitably for the show. I was, however, in the midst of a 60-hour alcohol binge that was about to enter its second peak, and was able to buy a giant Long Island Iced Tea cup for the show. I remember the whole show but I'm admittedly hazy on the subtleties of the plot. There was lots of in-line skating involved.

Near the end of the show, the all-Beatles soundtrack goes into an epic climax. They really back-loaded it: "Revolution," "Back in the U.S.S.R.," "While My Guitar Gently Weeps," "A Day in the Life," "Hey Jude," "Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band (Reprise)," All You Need Is Love." Lots of heavy hitters and widely known favorites in there. Anyway, for the whole show I'm struggling to maintain normalcy, instinctually going into air drumming and trying as hard as possible not to sing along, all while making spastic faces of ecstasy that rival mental instability. After a while, I do a pretty good job of doing this.

But then "A Day in the Life" started.

When the orchestra built up, and the pianos came down, and the arena went dark and silent, I let out the loudest yelp in human history. I can't really tell whether it was an involuntary explosion or if I honestly expected the entire crowd to go as fucking crazy as I was. All I know is that I was PUMPED. It was one of those moments where you're watching it happen as it unravels, as though your consciousness and body have separated, and you're watching from afar wondering "what the fuck could I have possibly been thinking there?" And yet, you can't stop yourself; you can only stand and watch the carnage unfurl.

My friends were mad at me. I'm really sorry but it doesn't change what happened. And to make matters worse, the crowd stayed completely silent for, like, another 30 seconds, and all you could hear was embarrassed, stifled nervous laughter coming from our row. I can't tell if the performers would have been angry or amused if they'd heard that. I like to think they loved it.
