Now the faculty has to bare the brunt of the storm, like it's their fault. That and probably attrition through retirement, then replacing with underpaid and benefit-less adjuncts.



I think one reason is the market for a private commuter school is shrinking. Going on-line is difficult and costly. I would guess it would be tough to beat Temple on price, and Lasalle has been quite good at serving non-trads for a long time. I don't think St. Joes was trying to become "elite," I think they were trying to survive by becoming a poor man's Villanova. The problem is that going forward, even universities like Villanova may struggle. You might be willing to pay for real prestige at say Penn or Swarthmore, or you might choose to get an affordable quality education at West Chester or Temple or Penn State. Bros from wealthy families will continue to provide revenue for places like Nova. I think a reasonably well endowed second tier place like Lehigh or Lafayette will do well enough also though even Haverford and Bryn Mawr have had some challenges. Below that, there is going to be a shake out in private schools. Schools are doing all kinds of questionable things in order to survive--why else would Drexel be starting up a law school? Because 10 years ago, law schools were seen as a surefire way to print money. Alas, that bubble did in fact burst.
