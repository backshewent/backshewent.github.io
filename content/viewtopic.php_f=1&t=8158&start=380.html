by lightsout54 » Wed Apr 15, 2009 22:57:26

This is something I posted to friends the other day when i was really struggling through the memorials.


4-13-2009

“Did the Phillies win today?” was the question I was confronted with.

I could only nod in affirmation as my lips couldn’t bring the words to surface.

That was probably because despite the box score that read a 9-8 victory for the Phils over the Washington Nationals, the reality is that they suffered one of the greatest losses in the 126 year history of the franchise.

Harry Kalas died today at the age of 73.

In passing, he took with him a thunderously smooth baritone voice, a wonderful love of the game and the soul of a franchise and fan base that is left speechless in his absence.

Through good times and bad Kalas was the epitome of professionalism. In Philadelphia, the land of 10,000 losses, there have been quite a bit of bad times. What Kalas brought however, was the unique ability to make every game seem like it was the most important game on the schedule, every tight game feel like game 7 of the World Series.

Its times like these that fans carry with them heavy hearts and perhaps even teary eyes. To many it seems silly to mourn a man that they may have never even met, but to some the loss is akin to that of family or friends. You see throughout the years, friendships develop and deteriorate, families can grow apart and people come and go. However, for nearly forty years, Harry Kalas was in your living room and on your radio. Fathers who sat with sons on their lap give way to sons who sit with grandchildren on theirs. Same team. Same voice . Same home run call. Same charm.

The signs of spring usher in hope and new beginnings. However, along with each new budding branch comes the gentle warmth of familiarity. The robin sings outside your window, the sun shares a golden glow a few hours longer, and that same friendly voice brings to you a new season of the game. The game you played as a boy and escaped to as a man, if only chase boyhood once more . 162 more times you would meet again and pull for your Phils . The names on the jerseys may change, the man behind the mic never did.

Kalas and his longtime broadcast partner, Richie “Whitey” Ashburn, were fixtures in living rooms up until Ashburn’s passing in 1997. In an era of free agency filled contracts, million dollar men and thousand dollar seats it is easy to grow bitter, to turn your back on the game like a page in a scorecard. Not when Harry was at the helm. You could not help but find yourself immersed in the game when it was called by a man with the same exuberance of child who had gone through the turn style hoping for a tip of the cap from his favorite player. It’s easy over the years to get beaten down by the business of life. Harry freed us from this and brought back to us the love of the game.

The golden mic has fallen silent and summers will never be the same. Baseball diamonds are forever but legends, sadly, are not. Philly’s finest is “outta here”. Ten thousand losses… none more painful than this.
