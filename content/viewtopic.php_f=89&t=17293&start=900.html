Werthless wrote:
Grotewold wrote:This Bill James article on how to stop tanking has a very sharp Hinkie take imo

It reduces interference with the playoff race in the last few weeks of the season (by disregarding the last 20 games), and also helps the teams with multiple years of ineptitude. You won't get a Tim Duncan going to an already loaded team.

The way to reduce tanking is simple: don't make the draft the most efficient way of getting talent. IMO, you can go two ways with it:

1) Raise salaries on draft picks. As of now, not only is the draft your best chance to acquire a superstar, but it's also the most financially adventageous. This year is a pretty crazy aberration, so I'll use last year's cap as an example. The top pick, Karl-Anthony Towns, got a starting salary of $5.7 million, which is 8.1% of the $70 million cap. (This year's it's going to be like 6%, which is nuts, but again, an aberration.) Increase the financial risk on a draft pick to a higher level, and I think it might at least scare a couple teams off.

2) Eliminate the max. The bottom line is that while you have the max, small market destinations that don't already have a superstar in place might as well not show up. All things equal, elite talent aren't going to play in Milwaukee. However, if you give Milwaukee the opportunity to offer $40 million when nobody else has that much cap space, then you might be in the game. Eliminating the max comes with a host of other issues, and I don't necessarily think it would make the game better, but again, if you're really that concerned about tanking, you're going to have to take something from somebody else or create some serious competitive balance issues. You can do quick fix reforms, but don't come out and act surprised when attendance is at an all-time low and Charlotte's not approving a new arena after 10 straight losing seasons
