by jeff2sf » Tue Aug 28, 2012 15:15:33

The Dude wrote:but just your tip doesn't pay for it. It's everyone that tips the server, so that all adds up to deflect the food costs.


But doesn't that add up the same way? Let's say a restaurant only gets 3 customers a day and sells two items, one that costs 10 bucks, one that's 20. And we add the tip -

Customer 1 orders the 20 dollar thing. He tips 25%. He pays 25 bucks
Customer 2 orders the 10 dollar thing, he tips 15%. He pays 11.5 bucks
Customer 3 orders both, he tips 20%. She pays 36 bucks.

Now let's say the restaurant gets rid of tipping and raises food prices 20%.

Customer 1 would pay 24 bucks now, saving 1 dollar... which he'd probably give back to the server over and above.
Customer 2 would pay 12 dollars, costing 50 cents. He gets hurt.
Customer 3 would pay 36 dollars... she's even.

So I don't see what you're saying unless the restaurant raises prices beyond your normal tipping level.

Re: Clever Random Thoughts Titles Are for Sissies

by Phan In Phlorida » Tue Aug 28, 2012 16:36:31

Some factors to consider:

Restaurants have extended non-peak periods each day with few, if any, customers. While restauranteurs stagger shifts, skeletal wait staff, have wait staff perform other tasks, etc during these slow periods of any given day, they're still paying salary.

Minimum wage for "tipped workers" averages somewhere around $2-$3 per hour, so you're looking at a 300%-400% increase just to get it to "regular" minimum wage. To around the level of a typical daily "take", around a 2000% salary increase.

The math isn't as clear-cut as 15%-20% of average meal since volume/revenue would favor chain and high-end restaurants, with small and startups at a disadvantage. Not to mention external economic factors.

While there is an x% of food cost formula for setting meal costs, a lot of restaurant startups fail because they don't get the math right (find the right balance to weather slow days).
