by jerseyhoya » Mon Dec 31, 2018 17:31:54

With the caveat that I've never been more wrong about anything in my life than assessing how the presidential race would go in 2016, here are my thoughts reading the past page or two.

1) No Dem is unelectable. If you learned one lesson from 2016, it should be any major party nominee has a realistic shot at being president.

2) The likability, popularity, policy positions, race, gender, etc. of the individual nominated by the Democratic Party will have less of an impact on the outcome of which party wins in 2020 than it did in 2016. Beyond being unpopular, Hillary had to contend with the fact that she was the de facto incumbent, which manifested itself in voters who viewed both Trump and Hillary unfavorably voting for Trump by a wide margin. The 2020 election is going to be first and foremost about Trump. If the economy stays good and Trump survives Mueller and behaves like an adult for the two weeks before the election, he may be a solid favorite regardless of who the Dems run. If he keeps taking on water and the economy cools, he's going to have a very difficult time of winning regardless of who the Dems run.

3) Some people are more electable than other people. I think TV is right that party leader types may not be the best judge of who has the magical electability pixie dust and who doesn't, but that doesn't mean it isn't real. We did some stuff earlier this month in a few key swing states and Biden was up 11% on Trump aggregated while Warren was only up 1%. Some of that is down to name ID, and I also think Biden will be less popular once he becomes a candidate again rather than the out of the fray elder statesman role he's currently in, but it sure seems right now of the well known candidates (him, Sanders, Warren) he is best positioned to beat Trump. Harder to know how many of the 2nd and 3rd tier types will do until they join the fray both to see how they handle the national spotlight and to get their recognition with voters high enough that people have meaningful opinions.

4) No one is voting for another year, and most Dem voters (as opposed to people like ourselves who spend far too much time online or otherwise following politics) aren't going to engage with the race for a while and tons will change over the course of 2019.

5) If Trump isn't the GOP nominee, throw out most of what I just wrote though #1 should hold. Depending on why he isn't the GOP nominee and who is the GOP nominee, different Democrats might be better positioned to win than those who would be in best shape to beat Trump.
