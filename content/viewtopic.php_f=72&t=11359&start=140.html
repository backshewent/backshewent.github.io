Fundraising is so tough because you've got a bunch of companies really marking up everyday items. As someone who comparison shops everything, I'd rather just write a check and skip buying the crap.

I've got this on the brain because the kids' school has really changed up fundraising in the last year. I'm impressed because a new cadre came into the PTA and basically said "we're tired of the old way". They dropped 95% of the wrapping paper/candy/etc. They found that another way to make an old stand-by fundraiser more profitable (by ~$3000) just by dropping one part of it. Then they brought it new items that require less footwork and are more interesting, yet bring in more money.

This year they're raffling off a new car at their carnival and a week at an oceanfront beach house.

by TenuredVulture » Mon Oct 11, 2010 15:58:27

The other issue is that a long time ago, the scouts or whoever would actually sell the crap themselves. Now, the office is a minefield of people with kids who have their hands out for overpriced shit you really don't want.

At least with Girl Scout cookies you can get away with spending $3.50. And they really do emphasize having the girls do the selling, even if paranoia has put the kibosh on door to door selling.

But yeah, I think for most groups just asking for money would work better--I know that's how it worked when I was a kid with our baseball and football teams. Give me $5 and I'll leave you alone!

My wife is Executive Director of our local Arts Council and one of their most successful fundraisers was a bull drop--basically, they laid out a grid over a parking lot and people bought squares for $50, and if the bull dropped its load on your square you won $1000 or something like that. I thought it was kind of tacky for an arts fundraiser, but I was obviously wrong. They had a band there and it turned out to be pretty fun.
