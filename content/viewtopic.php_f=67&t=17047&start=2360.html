my opinion on this has been reshaped over the years, especially in the aftermath of the paterno/sandusky thing. it all sounds great in theory, but when you actually see these kids' dreams crushed because of something that may or may not have truly happened so many years ago is upsetting. further, comparing syracuse to north carolina is lazy and shows that you don't actually know what happened or is happening at either school. when this group of kids stepped foot on campus, there were no NCAA issues.

and if you do not think every major program isn't sleezy, you're naive and haven't paid attention to the news over the past 5-6 years. football/basketball are billion dollar industries; industries that pay for several other loss-leading athletic programs and academic budgets. the leaders at these colleges care about the bottom line and that's all. it's a facade to think any of them give a damn about the student-athlete experience aside from ensuring the current racket continues on into perpetuity. not all of those leaders have a Harvard-sized endowment fund to leverage. i mean, it makes a ton of sense for WVU to be in the Big 12, right? and i'm sure ben simmons' academic issues are the exception, not the norm, for 1-and-done basketball players. how do the football players at big-time schools squeeze in class work when their full-time football job is in-season?

right, wrong, or indifferent, i'm tired of the kids getting punished. the unc-situation has provided quite a bit of context for me over the past few years. i don't think the current UNC basketball administration was at fault in any way, shape, or form. but admittedly, i remember growing up finding it funny how many guys in the Guthridge/Doherty era were African American History majors. Now, whether those guys were steered there purposefully or went that route knowing the classes were easy isn't 100% known, at least for the men's program. but to my point about all the colleges being sleezy, every university has GPA-boosting classes like that; every single one, including the private, D2 school I went to. and every semester, the 100+person class was filled with athletes. coincidence? no. athletic programs are the lifeblood on any university. they draw huge amounts of cash and alumni support. and if you don't have those two things as a leader of the university or the athletic program, you don't hold that job for very long.

Apologies for the ramble.

Asked Monday if he would have committed to UNC had it not faced such uncertainty because of the NCAA investigation, Ingram said, “I think I would have.”

“I liked them a lot,” he said. “But I’m kind of glad that I waited.”

Ingram’s father, Donald, said he and his family wanted answers to questions that, to this point, have been impossible to answer. It’s unclear how the NCAA investigation will affect the UNC men’s basketball program, and what kind of sanctions – if any – coach Roy Williams and his team will face.

The questions about what might happen at UNC – and what penalties it might face – proved too difficult for Williams and his staff to overcome during Ingram’s recruitment. Donald Ingram said the cloud of the NCAA investigation “played a big factor” in his son’s decision to turn down UNC.

“We wanted to see something on paper,” Donald Ingram said. “We wanted to hear it on television. We wanted to know that they’re not going to fall into the same situation like Jim Boeheim with Syracuse. So you don’t want to go into a (situation) that’s already hot. And it played a factor in it.”

An eight-year investigation into the Syracuse men’s basketball program found an assortment of violations, and the sanctions, which the NCAA announced last month, included scholarship cuts and the suspension of Boeheim from his team’s first nine ACC games next season.
